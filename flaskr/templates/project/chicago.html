Summary

The relationship between socioeconomic status and crime is an important topic for public policy. In this project, we conducted a statistical analysis using data from the City of Chicago to explore the potential correlation between different dimensions of socioeconomic status and crime rates at the community-level. By running a series of regressions, we found that it is not the total number of crime but the type of crime that is correlated to the socioeconomic factors, and among the seven selected socioecomonic indicators, the crowded housing rate and the unemployment rate seems to matter more than the others.

Motivation

Chicago has long been criticized for its high crime rate. However, when we broke down Chicago's total number of crime to the communities, we found out a striking disparity among communities. Except Montclaire, the next top five communites have an average of 11,850.6 crimes per year, comparing to 656.5 in the lowest five communities on average.

Among all the seventy-seven communties, Monclaire appears to be an outlier, enjoying a number of crime per year almost twice as large as the second-ranked community, and about 67 occurrence of crime per day.

On the Wikipedia page for Montcliar, Chicago, the median household income is $6,238 lower than the average level in Chicago, and 24.4% of the residents there earned less than $25,000 per year. The unemployment rate is 12.3%, comparing to 5% at the city-level.

We suspect that the demographic features of the community could partially explain the extreme high amount of crime in the area. And we would like to systematically examine the relationship between the socioecomonic indicators and the number of crime at the community-level.

Study Design

Out goal for this project is to understand whether there is a correlation between a community's different crime rates and its socioecomonc status

We acquired the dataset from Chicago's government API containing 5-year American Community Servey data and some precalculated indexes by the Chicago Department of Public Health(CDPH).

The per capita income in the data set was estimated by dividing the aggregate income of the census tracts within the community area by the number of residents.

According to CDPH's report An Update on Urban Hardship The hardship index is a combined score of six selected socioeconomic indicators, ranging from 1 to 100, with the higher score representing a greater level of hardship. The scores are standardized according to the data for the 77 community areas, and therefore cannot be compared to scores generated for other jurisdictions.

The crime data set is provided by the Chicago Police Department and is recorded on a daily basis. In order to merge with the socioecomonic data set, we calculated the number of crime per year during 2008-2012.

Total Number of Crime

We started our statistical analysis from doing the regression's analysis (regplot) for the sum of all kinds of crimes VS. hardship index from socioeconomic status indicators. The result was amazing, basically nothing. The regression line is almost horizontal. This was not expected.

We then proceeded to plot the sum of all kinds of crimes VS. all other six different kinds of socioeconomic status indicators. Result is shown below:

It seems that different socioecomonic aspects have no statistically significant effect on the incidence of crime.

We then took absolute value and the log transformation the input data and measured them against the crimes rates in percentage and count. No result is found to be statically significant.

Our guess for this was that the correlations were embedded at a more granular geographical level. Unfortunately there was no data available in census block group or smaller geographical units so we could not validate this theory.

Crimes by Primary Type

We picked 7 more frequent kinds crimes out of the 30 in total. They are

  • Homicide
  • Weapons Violation
  • Theft
  • Kidnapping
  • Narcotics
  • Robbery
  • Sex Offense

We picked 6 features as predictors for this regressional study

  • crowd: percentage of housing crowded
  • poverty: percentage of households below poverty
  • unemployed: percentage of age 16+ unemployed
  • highschool: percentage of age 25+ without high school degree
  • working: percentage of people between age 18 and 64
  • income: per capita income

Then we focused on each individual crime and took the log-transformation for the dataset to study their correlations

Crime Type Significant Predictors

The result shows that, most of the time, with only Unemployed or Housing Crowded significant, other factors we conventionally think are important, are actually not important. For example, I was personally very interested in the Per Capita Income, but it was never ever significant in our analysis.

One possible analysis is that most of the crimes in Chicago are related to gangs. Being in a gang, you are in it full-time. So you have to be unemployed to be in a gang, or get involved in crimes. Higher percentage in Housing Crowded means higher likelihood for frictions among the tribes and gangs. They are more prone to conflicts. For my personal favorite, Per Capita Income, members in gangs may actually have very good income, which offsets the general 'low income, high crime rate' trend.
